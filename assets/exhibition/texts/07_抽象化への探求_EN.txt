B. In Search of Abstraction

Abstraction refers to the act of extracting shared attributes from many objects, matters, or concrete concepts, and understanding them as a general concept. However, making architecture cannot be completed through such pure concepts alone.

Rather than finding attributes shared by the objects themselves, when the maker becomes aware that their own methods of observation and expression are what the depicted objects have in common, the resulting expression may deviate from common sense.

For example, in Paul Cézanne, Pommes et oranges, (right), the patterns and forms of different objects appear to intersect and merge with one another. This is likely because Cézanne’s own way of seeing the world is revealed there. If Cézanne had not been conscious of the act of looking at the subject, this expression of objects merging with one another would not have emerged.

In deviated expressions, there exist the maker’s own perceived fragments and interpretations. There are moments when things that had appeared uniform and social begin to look complex and private. Interpretation is private rather than general, but it can become an opportunity to reconsider, in multiple ways, subjects that we tend to assume have only one meaning.

Deviated expression through interpretation is something carried out only by the maker, within the domain of the work as something separated from the real world. Abstraction begins when the maker acknowledges that they are inherent within the work.

If we were to redefine the shared attributes among many objects and concrete matters in abstraction, they would not be universal characteristics existing within the things themselves, but rather the maker’s own way of seeing the world.
